# Navigating the Information Cascade

### A Multi-Modal Simulation Framework for AI Literacy, Transformative Learning, and Prosocial Complexity

**Karen Doore** Humanity++ · Bridging Spiral SR2 March 2026

_Draft for workshop circulation — not for formal citation without author permission_

***

> _"The question is not whether AI systems will transform human cognition. They already have. The question is whether that transformation will be extractive or generative — and whether educational frameworks exist that can help people tell the difference."_

### Abstract

We are living through a rapid and largely undesigned transformation of the human information environment. AI systems now mediate the majority of information flows in economically developed societies, operating at speeds, scales, and levels of personalisation that exceed the adaptive capacity of most existing educational frameworks. The result is a widening gap between the technical sophistication of information systems and the cognitive, somatic, and relational capacities that humans require to engage with those systems wisely.

This paper introduces the **Bridging Spiral SR2 framework** — a multi-modal simulation model for AI literacy that draws on convergent findings from computational neuroscience, complex systems theory, contemplative science, relational neuroscience, and prosocial economics. The framework proposes that genuine AI literacy requires not merely technical knowledge but the development of four integrated capacities — somatic awareness, cognitive discernment, relational accountability, and temporal integration — operationalised as navigational instruments within an interactive simulation environment.

The framework makes a specific claim: the transition from extractive to generative information dynamics — at the level of individuals, communities, and potentially civilisations — is a phase transformation in the thermodynamic sense. It requires sufficient energy input in the form of productive disequilibration, held within a field of kindness as a regulatory condition, to overcome the activation barrier between attractor states. The simulations developed in this project are designed to make this transition legible, encounter-able, and — through repeated embodied engagement — learnable.

The paper describes the theoretical grounding, the simulation architecture, the pedagogical rationale, and the honest limitations of the framework at its current stage of development. It concludes with an invitation to collaboration and a proposal for the next phase of research.

### 1. The Problem: AI Literacy in a VUCA World

#### 1.1 The Attention Economy and Its Successor

The attention economy — the system in which human attention is the primary commodity, captured through recommendation algorithms, notification design, and personalised content streams — is well documented in its pathological effects. Reduced attention spans, epistemic fragmentation, affective polarisation, and the suppression of reflective cognition have been measured across multiple populations and research contexts.

What is less discussed is that we are now transitioning from the attention economy to what some analysts call the **experience economy**: a system in which AI tools shape not merely what people see but how they feel, what they remember, what they believe is possible, and ultimately who they understand themselves to be. The stakes are categorically higher. The design principles are largely absent.

In the Volatile, Uncertain, Complex, and Ambiguous (VUCA) information environment that results, most people — including most educators — lack the navigational frameworks needed to distinguish between information flows that build human capacity and information flows that extract it. This is not a failure of intelligence. It is a failure of educational design. The tools do not yet exist, at scale, that can teach these distinctions in ways that are embodied, encounter-able, and emotionally accessible.

#### 1.2 What Existing AI Literacy Frameworks Miss

Current AI literacy initiatives typically address three domains: technical understanding (how AI systems work), critical evaluation (how to detect misinformation), and ethical awareness (how to reason about AI impacts). These are necessary. They are not sufficient.

What is missing is what we might call the **somatic layer**: the embodied, affective, and relational capacities through which a person actually processes information in real time — not in a classroom exercise but in the charged encounter with a social media feed at 11pm, or in a meeting room where the recommendation algorithm's preferred narrative is the dominant voice, or in the moment when one's own community is mobilising warmth and solidarity in ways that are simultaneously genuine and exclusionary.

Technical knowledge about how recommendation algorithms work does not, by itself, change how a person _responds_ when the algorithm is working on them. Emotional intelligence, somatic regulation, and the capacity to tolerate cognitive disequilibration — to remain open at the moment when the system's pull toward closure is strongest — these are the capacities that determine whether AI literacy knowledge becomes AI literacy practice.

#### 1.3 The Urgency

This paper is written at a moment of visible systemic dysfunction. AI capabilities are being concentrated in military, surveillance, and extractive commercial systems at a rate that significantly outpaces the development of corresponding educational, ethical, and regulatory frameworks. The anxiety this produces in populations — well-founded anxiety, grounded in real evidence of real harm — is itself a resource that extractive information systems exploit, driving further closure, further polarisation, and further retreat from the cognitive openness that transformative learning requires.

The urgency is real. It is also, in the framework's terms, a form of disequilibration — the productive instability that precedes transformation, if it can be held within a sufficient field of kindness and channelled through adequate navigational instruments.

This paper is an attempt to describe what those instruments might be.

### 2. Theoretical Foundations

The Bridging Spiral framework draws on six convergent research domains. This section provides a brief account of each and the specific contribution it makes to the framework.

#### 2.1 PSI Theory and the Suppression of Higher-Order Cognition

Personality Systems Interactions (PSI) Theory (Kuhl, Quirin & Koole, 2020) proposes a seven-level architecture of human agency, from basic sensorimotor habits to Self-Management. The theory's most important finding for the framework is the **regression mechanism**: under high stress or threat, cortisol attenuates the influence of higher-level motivational systems, causing behaviour to be governed by lower-level habits regardless of the person's stated intentions or values.

This finding grounds the framework's central claim about the DIKW stack: under dominance conditions — in systems designed around threat, comparison, and scarcity — access to Understanding and Wisdom is neurologically suppressed. The upper levels of the stack are not accessible through effort of will. They require the field condition that the framework calls **kindness** — the combination of psychological safety, warm truth, firm limits, and the possibility of repair.

The implication for AI literacy design: an educational approach that increases anxiety about AI — even accurately — may suppress the very cognitive capacities required to respond to that anxiety wisely.

#### 2.2 Active Inference and the Predictive Brain

The Free Energy Principle and Active Inference framework (Friston, 2010; Clark, 2016) proposes that living systems maintain their organisation by minimising the difference between their internal predictions and incoming sensory data. Systems can minimise this prediction error in two ways: by updating their internal models (learning) or by acting on the environment to make it match their predictions (control).

The balance between these strategies — between model updating and environmental control — is a critical parameter of the framework. Systems under threat predominantly adopt the control strategy: they act to eliminate the surprising information rather than integrate it. Systems under safety can afford model updating: they can tolerate surprise long enough to learn from it.

This provides a formal account of the **aperture** parameter in the framework: the learning rate — how much novel signal a system integrates per encounter — is not a stable cognitive trait but a dynamic response to the safety conditions of the information environment. Designing for aperture means designing for safety.

#### 2.3 Natural Born Intelligence and the Neither/Nor Mechanism

The Natural Born Intelligence (NBI) framework distinguishes biological intelligence from artificial intelligence by a specific structural feature: the capacity for **non-monotonic transformation of representational space**. AI systems, however sophisticated, operate within fixed representational spaces — they optimise and generalise within existing categories but cannot generate a genuine "outside" — a domain not contained in the original categorical structure.

Human intelligence can, under specific conditions, transform its own representational space: through the **Neither/Nor mechanism**, a system temporarily releases both category A and its negation, entering a vacant-place state from which new categories can emerge. This is not a mystical claim. It is a formal description of the cognitive operation that underlies genuine creativity, ethical growth, and transformative learning.

NBI research also identifies the signature of this capacity in physiological data: the **Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD)** of cardiac autonomic markers during creative engagement. High MAD co-occurring with active regulation — not relaxation, but energised stability — predicts transformative outcomes. This is what the framework calls the **Meditation Paradox**: the productive state is one of high arousal plus active regulation, not low-arousal calm.

#### 2.4 Self-Organised Criticality and the Edge of Chaos

Self-Organised Criticality (SOC) theory (Bak, 1996; Sbitnev, 2024) establishes that certain dynamical systems naturally evolve toward a critical state — the edge of chaos — at which point the system is maximally sensitive to perturbation and capable of responses of any magnitude. At SOC, small inputs can produce large effects: avalanches of any size become possible.

Applied to human learning and social systems, SOC provides the formal grounding for what the framework calls the **avalanche of kindness** — the theoretical possibility that, in a system operating at the edge of chaos, a well-timed, genuine act of care oriented toward opening rather than closing the representational space can produce a phase transition of any magnitude.

This is not naive idealism. It is a specific, theoretically grounded mechanism with empirical analogues in ecology (forest fire dynamics), neuroscience (critical branching in neural circuits), and social systems (threshold models of collective action). The framework does not claim this always happens. It claims it becomes possible in systems that have been brought to the critical state through sustained kindness, tolerated disequilibration, and generative resonance.

#### 2.5 Causal Emergence and Holarchic Organisation

Causal emergence theory (Hoel, Albantakis & Tononi, 2013) demonstrates mathematically that macro-level organisation can have greater causal power than the sum of its micro-level components. This formalises the intuition behind holarchic thinking: the whole is not merely the sum of its parts — it has independent causal efficacy that its components do not possess individually.

This finding is significant for the framework in two ways. First, it provides a formal justification for designing learning environments at the holarchic scale — not just individual cognitive interventions but shared information environments with collective properties. Second, it grounds the claim that the **values vector** — the direction of a system's loss function, what it is organised to maximise — operates at the macro level and cannot be reduced to individual agent behaviour. A system can be composed of individuals each behaving ethically and still have an extractive macro-level values vector. Literacy at the holarchic scale requires the capacity to perceive this.

#### 2.6 Prosocial Economics and Commitment Pooling

Grassroots Economics research on Community Inclusion Currencies identifies **Commitment Pooling** as a fundamental protocol observable in both mycorrhizal fungal networks and ancient human mutual-aid traditions. Distinguished from centralised exchange economies, commitment pooling creates non-zero-sum connections through an intangible common-pool of trusted promises — without requiring a central node to mediate exchange.

This provides the framework with a social-economic model for what genuine resonance looks like at scale — and distinguishes it from the apparently similar but structurally different dynamic of extractive centralisation (the Giant Pumpkin metaphor). The key distinction: in a commitment pool, each node retains genuine distinctness while contributing to shared abundance. In extractive centralisation, nodes progressively lose distinctness as their resources flow toward the centre.

### 3. The Framework Architecture

#### 3.1 MPCM: The Foundational Boundary

The framework is built on a four-layer model of human meaning-making:

**Material → Process → Context → Meaning**

AI systems — at their current state of development — reliably generate Material and facilitate Process. Context requires a living system embedded in time, relationship, and embodied experience. Meaning requires all of the above plus the somatic nonlinearity that the framework calls the activation function.

This boundary — between what AI systems can do and what requires a living system — is the central argument of the framework, and the central argument for the kind of AI literacy it proposes. The goal is not to teach people to distrust AI systems. It is to help people locate themselves clearly on the MPCM boundary: to know which operations they can appropriately delegate and which they cannot, without losing themselves in the delegation.

#### 3.2 The DIKW Stack as Navigation Instrument

The Data-Information-Knowledge-Understanding-Wisdom (DIKW) stack serves as the framework's primary navigation instrument — a map of the cognitive territory that AI-mediated information environments traverse.

The framework adds three specific refinements to the standard DIKW model:

**The ML threshold**: current AI systems reliably reach Knowledge — stable, validated models with strong generalisation capacity. This is marked in the simulations as a dashed boundary. Everything below this line is territory where AI is genuinely powerful. Everything above it requires the living system.

**The K→U threshold**: the most significant boundary in the stack, and the one most actively suppressed by dominance-oriented information environments. Understanding requires somatic engagement — the embodied contextualisation that the framework models as the nonlinear activation function. Without it, Knowledge accumulates but does not become Understanding; the person becomes progressively more informed and progressively less wise.

**The Desirable Difficulties Zone**: the productive discomfort at the K→U threshold — where the learner's existing models are insufficient for the situation at hand and the system must either expand or contract. Expanding requires tolerated disequilibration. Contracting requires threat elimination. The design challenge is to create conditions in which expansion is the path of least resistance.

#### 3.3 The Four Instruments

The framework proposes four integrated navigational instruments, each corresponding to a layer of the human cognitive-somatic architecture and to a structural element in deep neural network models:

**♠ Somatic Gyroscope** (Earth / Activation Function) Proprioceptive intelligence, window of tolerance, felt sense. The nonlinear activation function without which the entire architecture collapses to a single linear transformation regardless of its apparent depth. The first layer suppressed by stress, the first restored by safety and somatic practice.

**♦ Cognitive Radar** (Air / Linear Transformation) Formal cognition, categorical reasoning, metacognition, integrative epistemology. The scanning function — powerful and essential, but requiring the somatic layer for genuine depth. Without the somatic gyroscope, cognitive radar produces tunnel vision: isolated object detection without holistic pattern recognition.

**♥ Relational Compass** (Water / Loss Function) Prosocial values, value system coherence, circle of relationship, repair capacity. The instrument that determines the direction of the entire system's learning — what counts as error, what counts as success. The values vector. The most important single parameter for distinguishing genuine holarchy from extractive mimicry.

**♣ Dimensional Integration** (Fire / Distributed Temporal Learning) Integration across time, levels, relationships, and experience. Importantly: not modelled as backpropagation (which requires phase-switched global passes) but as continuous, local, state-dependent updating — predictive coding at the temporal scale. The capacity to carry learning forward without a central controller.

#### 3.4 The Three Meta-Parameters

Three cross-cutting parameters modulate the function of all four instruments:

**❄ Kindness**: field condition, not personality trait. Makes aperture possible by creating the safety conditions under which prediction-error minimisation through model-updating (learning) becomes more advantageous than through environmental control (defensiveness).

**✦ Imagination**: latent space access — the capacity to hold unrealised possibility. Has two modes: generative (recombination within existing representational space) and transformative (breaking the space itself through the Neither/Nor mechanism). Directionless — amplifies whatever the values vector is oriented toward.

**◈ Disequilibration**: the productive instability parameter. The Meditation Paradox operationalised: not the absence of disturbance but the tolerated presence of it. The parameter that distinguishes the maze (smooth flow within fixed categories, low MAD, apparent kindness) from genuine holarchy (crack-prone, high MAD, alive).

#### 3.5 The Shadow Parameter Problem

The most original contribution of the framework is the explicit modelling of shadow dynamics — the ways in which the same parameters that produce holarchic flow can produce sophisticated extractive systems when the values vector is misaligned.

The critical insight: **extractive mimicry is beautiful**. A well-optimised dominance system can exhibit high apparent kindness (in-group warmth), high apparent imagination (elaboration and creativity within fixed categories), and high apparent resonance (strong internal coherence). It is indistinguishable from genuine holarchy from within. It is only distinguishable from the outside — or from the fracture points where genuine difference is excluded.

The minimum set of distinguishing features:

1. **The crack**: does disequilibration open the representational space, or is it contained within it?
2. **The holonomy**: does the system exit the crack in a new geometric regime, or does it return to its prior configuration?
3. **The values vector**: does the system's loss function point toward care for the outside, or toward care that closes the outside?
4. **The MAD signature**: is volatility tolerated and integrated, or suppressed and punished?

The Pass 3 simulation (Giant Pumpkin · Commitment Pool) makes these distinctions encounter-able through interactive parameter exploration.

### 4. The Simulation Architecture

#### 4.1 Rationale for Interactive Simulation as Learning Medium

The choice of interactive simulation as the primary pedagogical medium is not incidental. It follows from the framework's core claims:

If genuine learning requires the Neither/Nor mechanism — a temporary suspension of existing categories — then the pedagogical form must create conditions for that suspension rather than prevent it. Lecture and text are poorly suited to this: they deliver categories rather than creating the productive encounter with their limits.

Interactive simulation creates a different kind of encounter: the learner adjusts parameters and observes dynamics, develops hypotheses, is surprised, revises. The system talks back. The learner's existing models are tested against behaviour they did not predict. This is the desirable difficulty zone in action — and it occurs without the learner needing to know the theoretical apparatus that underlies it.

The 8-10 second transition in the DIKW Toroid simulation — the time it takes for the toroid to heal after dominance is released — is not a design choice that can be adequately described. It must be experienced. The duration carries the argument. This is what simulations can do that text cannot.

#### 4.2 The Simulation Series

The framework is currently implemented as seven interactive simulations, each expressing a different facet of the framework:

**Markov Blanket v1 and v2**: The boundary of self as permeable membrane. Mode 0 (Drift) vs Mode 1 (Inquiry). The difference between passive absorption and chosen aperture.

**Deep Architecture / Dashboard Dials**: The four instruments as a neural architecture. The suppression demonstration: remove the somatic layer and watch the architecture collapse.

**DIKW Toroid Pass 1**: The DIKW stack as a particle field on a torus. Dominance collapses the toroid. Holarchic flow restores circulation. The entropy/SOC waveform.

**DIKW Toroid Pass 2**: Full parameter engagement. Active inference readout. Desirable difficulties threshold. Emotion as navigation signal. Nested holarchic scales.

**Human ↔ AI Coupling**: Two toroids across a charged gap. The MPCM boundary made intimate. The AI never changes. Only the human transforms.

**DIKW Toroid Pass 3 — Giant Pumpkin · Commitment Pool**: Closed space vs open space. The crack between them. The one question.

All simulations are publicly accessible at: **https://kdoore.github.io/HumanityPlusPlus**

#### 4.3 The One Question

All six simulations orbit a single pedagogical question, made explicit in Pass 3:

> _Which of these does the information environment you live in most resemble — and what would it take to change that?_

The first part creates the diagnostic encounter. The second part assumes agency — not just recognition but the beginning of a response. Together they constitute the minimum viable AI literacy intervention: one question that a learner cannot honestly answer without exercising the capacities the framework is designed to develop.

### 5. The Technosocial Phase Transformation Hypothesis

This section makes the framework's largest claim explicitly and humbly.

#### 5.1 The Claim

Complex systems can undergo phase transitions — rapid reorganisations from one attractor state to another — when sufficient energy is introduced at the critical point. Water becomes ice at a specific temperature. A forest at SOC produces an avalanche from a single falling branch. A social movement at threshold produces collective action from a single catalytic event.

The hypothesis is this: the current global information ecosystem is operating near — but not at — a critical threshold. The combination of AI-amplified extractive dynamics, visible institutional dysfunction, and widespread AI anxiety constitutes a form of forced disequilibration without sufficient kindness field conditions to make it productive. The system is being destabilised without the regulatory conditions that would allow the destabilisation to be transformative rather than traumatic.

The educational framework proposed here is not the solution to this problem. It is a contribution to the kindness field condition — a tool for helping individuals and communities develop the navigational capacity to hold disequilibration productively, to distinguish genuine holarchy from its extractive mimics, and to act from the values vector oriented toward care.

If enough learners develop this capacity — if the Commitment Pool attractor becomes, in enough information environments, the more attractive option — then the conditions for a technosocial phase transformation become more probable. Small causes can have large effects in systems at SOC.

#### 5.2 The Humility

The hypothesis is theoretical. The simulations are demonstrations, not evidence. No study has yet measured whether learners who use these tools develop measurably different AI literacy capacities than learners who receive conventional education. No longitudinal data exists on whether simulated encounters with the Giant Pumpkin / Commitment Pool distinction transfer to recognition of those dynamics in real information environments.

These are the next research questions. This paper does not answer them. It is the argument for why they are worth asking.

#### 5.3 The SWOT Consideration

If this framework does not hold muster — if its theoretical grounding is insufficient, its pedagogical claims unsupported, its simulations too abstract to produce genuine transfer — then the question remains: what educational framework can drive prosocial transformation in AI-augmented information environments?

The absence of convincing alternatives is not an argument for this framework. But it is an argument for taking the question seriously. The information environment is not going to become less complex, less AI-mediated, or less VUCA. The educational systems that will help people navigate it need to be built. The question is what they will be built from.

This paper offers one set of building materials, grounded in convergent empirical research, expressed through interactive tools that are publicly available and freely replicable. It is an invitation to build alongside, not a claim of completion.

### 6. Experience Design as a Discipline

One implication of the framework that deserves its own brief articulation: **transformative experience design** should be recognised as a distinct discipline.

The confluence of AI-assisted content generation, interactive simulation technology, contemplative neuroscience, trauma-informed pedagogy, and prosocial complexity science creates the conditions for a new design practice — one that is explicitly oriented toward designing experiences that are:

* **Transformative by design**: structured to create the conditions for genuine representational space transformation, not merely the accumulation of content
* **Trauma-informed by design**: structured around the window of tolerance, with explicit attention to the kindness field conditions that make disequilibration productive rather than retraumatising
* **Holarchic by design**: structured to develop agency at multiple scales simultaneously — individual, relational, community, ecosystem — without collapsing any level into the others

The Bridging Spiral framework is a prototype for this discipline. The simulations are its first design artefacts. The studio practice — the physical environment, the contemplative disciplines, the somatic work, the collaborative relationships — is its embodied infrastructure.

The discipline needs a name, a community of practice, shared methodological standards, and a body of research on its effects. This paper is a step toward convening that community.

### 7. Limitations and Next Steps

**Current limitations:**

The framework is a conceptual integration, not a validated intervention. The simulations demonstrate the concepts but have not been tested for learning transfer. The research grounding is substantial but spans disciplines with different methodological standards, creating claims that are harder to evaluate than claims within a single discipline. The most powerful effects of the framework may require significant prior scaffolding — contemplative practice, somatic work, academic preparation — that limits its immediate accessibility to general populations.

**Next research priorities:**

1. Design and conduct a pilot study measuring AI literacy outcomes for learners who use the simulation series versus a comparison group
2. Develop entry-point materials that make the framework accessible without requiring prior scaffolding — the one question and the Giant Pumpkin image as starting points
3. Build the physical studio prototype: a designed contemplative creative space in which the simulations are one element alongside physical materials, moving light, and somatic practice
4. Develop the white paper into a peer-reviewed article, with explicit falsifiability conditions for the framework's core claims
5. Convene a small working group of researchers, artists, educators, and contemplative practitioners to stress-test and extend the framework

### 8. Conclusion

The information cascade is already here. AI systems are already mediating the majority of human information flows in economically developed societies. The question of whether that mediation will be extractive or generative — whether it will suppress or develop human cognitive, somatic, and relational capacity — is being answered now, in the design decisions of platforms, the training choices of AI systems, and the educational frameworks that do or do not prepare people to engage with both wisely.

This paper has described a framework for the latter: a multi-modal simulation model that makes the difference between extractive and generative information dynamics encounter-able, legible, and learnable. It is grounded in convergent empirical research. It is expressed through publicly available interactive tools. It is honest about its limitations and explicit about what would move it from hypothesis to evidence.

The framework rests on a specific and testable claim: that the capacity to distinguish the Giant Pumpkin from the Commitment Pool — to recognise extractive mimicry even when it is beautiful, to locate the crack, to tolerate the vacant-place state long enough for something genuinely new to emerge — is a learnable skill. And that learning it, at sufficient scale, changes the probability of the technosocial phase transformation toward regenerative, sustainable, holistic futures.

The one question remains patient, in the corner of the screen:

_Which of these does the information environment you live in most resemble — and what would it take to change that?_
